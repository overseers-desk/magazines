# like-human-do

A follow-up to someone you met and got on with is the next line of a relationship, not a business message. Write it as a person who liked them. The thing that betrays the operator is an ask wrapped in warmth; a real reader smells it at once, however warm the wrapping. Polishing the prose does not remove it, because the want is in the structure, not the words. So this is compose-time work: do it before you draft, not after.

## The ask: name it, then place it

Before writing, say plainly what this email wants from the recipient, and sort it:

- **A grab** takes value for the sender and hands the recipient a task or a pitch: a referral pipeline, a listing, a sale, "point a few our way and we'll do the same". Cut it. Do not dress it as warmth. If a grab is the only honest reason for the email, there is no human note to send; say so to the user instead of writing one.
- **A genuine ask** honours the recipient and costs them little: their opinion, their expertise, a small favour only they can do. Keep it, but humble and bare. Ask because you respect what they know ("you understand hotels far better than we do"), not with a case for why it serves your strategy. One plain sentence. No "what we want is", no positioning, no selling the value back to them.

When in doubt, weight the email toward giving: an invitation, a thing they would enjoy, help that costs you. A note that wants nothing is trusted; a note that wants something is read for the want. Even a necessary ask lands better with a real give beside it.

## The connection point

Before writing, list the concrete things this person actually said or shared that were *not* business: a story, a thing they are proud of, a side topic, something you both noticed. If one is genuinely memorable, lead with it or build on it. It beats the business takeaway, which is the salient thing in your memory and the wrong thing to reach for. Pick the one you would mention if you bumped into them in the street.

Mention it the way you would in passing: a glancing reference to the thing you both remember. You do not need to retell it back to them, and do not pronounce a verdict on their own affairs or agree hard with them about it. A light touch is warm; a re-narration with an opinion attached reads as flattery and as bonding to soften them up.

If there is no real personal moment with *this* recipient, do not force one, and never bend a detail about other people or other events into a closeness that was not there. A forced or strained connection reads worse than none. When there is none, write a sincere, specific, plain note about the actual reason you are writing, and say plainly that you have not met if you have not. Thank them only for what they themselves did. Use only what you would know from having met; never a fact you looked up about them.

## The rest

- Do not rank or grade the encounter ("the most useful thing I took", "well worth it", "stuck with me").
- Keep it short. A few lines. Length and polish read as effort, and effort reads as wanting something. A short note also costs the reader less to answer, which is what gets a reply.
- One true piece of yourself if you have it (a forward plan, a small disclosure), but only if real. Never invent one to seem warm.
- Greet and sign as a person: first name in, first name out, no title block, no "let us keep in touch".
- Plain words, the way you actually speak. No em dash, no balanced two-part flourishes, nothing that reads composed.
