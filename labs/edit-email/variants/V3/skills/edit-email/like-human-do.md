# like-human-do

An email is written to do something: to move the reader to act, or to keep a relationship warm. Make it read as a person wrote it, but never at the cost of the job it came to do. Warmth that drops the thing that does the job leaves a pleasant, useless note.

## The goal (work this out first)

Before you touch the words, say plainly what this email needs the recipient to do after reading it: reply, agree to a meeting, take up an offer, review something, or nothing at all (a true note of thanks or news). Infer it from the draft and the context. If the draft already states the ask, you have it; do not ask the user. Ask the user only when the intended action is genuinely unclear and you cannot infer it.

Then name what in the draft does the work of getting that action: the reason the recipient would say yes, the credibility the ask or offer rests on. That content is goal-bearing. Everything else is packaging.

## Their side (why they would care)

Work out why the recipient would want this, from their position, not yours. Where the email follows something they recently did, ask what they were trying to achieve by doing it; if the context points you to where that is written (a prior meeting, a thread), read it and reason from it. Then shape the offer as serving what they are trying to do, for the people they answer to. Do not recite their motive back to them as a claim; let the shaping show you understood it. An offer cut to their goal is accepted; one cut to yours is merely considered.

## Protect what does the job

When you cut for warmth and brevity, protect the goal-bearing content. The achievement that makes an offer worth accepting, the proof the sender can deliver, the single fact that earns the yes: keep it, even as you strip the rest. Cut only what is there for status and does not earn the action. The test is not "does this read as a boast" but "does the recipient need this to want to say yes." A fact that proves the offer is worth their people's time stays; the same fact waved only for standing goes.

## The ask: name it, then place it

A follow-up to someone you met is the next line of a relationship, not a pitch. The thing that betrays the operator is an ask wrapped in warmth; a real reader smells it, however warm the wrapping. Name what the email wants and sort it:

- **A grab** takes value for the sender and hands the recipient a task or a pitch: a referral pipeline, a listing, a sale. Cut it; do not dress it as warmth.
- **A genuine ask** honours the recipient and costs them little: their opinion, their expertise, a small favour only they can do. Keep it, humble and bare. Ask because you respect what they know, not with a case for why it serves your strategy. No "what we want is", no positioning.
- **An offer** gives the recipient or their group something useful: your help, your experience, a session for their members. This is the strongest kind of email and the easiest to mistake for a grab, because the sender benefits indirectly. Do not cut it; let it carry the email. The achievement behind the offer, the proof it is worth accepting, is goal-bearing: keep it (see "Protect what does the job"). Strip only status-flex that does not earn the offer, a boast unconnected to why they should say yes. The same fact can be either; decide by whether the recipient needs it to want the offer, not by whether it sounds like a brag.

A note that wants nothing is trusted; a note that wants something is read for the want. Even a necessary ask lands better with a real give beside it.

## The connection point

Before writing, list the concrete things this person actually said or shared that were *not* business: a story, a thing they are proud of, a side topic. If one is genuinely memorable, lead with it or build on it. It beats the business takeaway. Pick the one you would mention if you bumped into them in the street.

Mention it the way you would in passing: a glancing reference to the thing you both remember. Do not retell it back to them, and do not pronounce a verdict on their own affairs or agree hard with them about it. A light touch is warm; a re-narration with an opinion attached reads as flattery.

If there is no real personal moment with *this* recipient, do not force one, and never bend a detail about other people or events into a closeness that was not there. When there is none, write a sincere, plain note about the actual reason you are writing. Thank them only for what they themselves did. Use only what you would know from having met; never a fact you looked up.

## The opening

The reader already opened the email and already knows who you are. You are past the headline, so do not write one. Open with the plain real thing: thank them for the actual event, name the genuine shared moment, or say why you are writing. A general observation engineered to catch attention is a newspaper lede pointed at someone already reading; it reads as performance and the reader feels handled. A genuine shared specific stated plainly is welcome; an abstract hook engineered to land is not.

## Does it still do its job

Put your version beside the original and ask one question: is the reader at least as likely to do the intended thing? If your warmer, shorter version is less likely to get the action because it lost what earned it, you have over-corrected. Restore the goal-bearing piece and cut elsewhere. A human note that no longer does its job is a worse failure than a slightly stiff one that does.

## The rest

- Do not rank or grade the encounter ("the most useful thing I took", "well worth it", "stuck with me").
- Be short, and cut for reply-price. Every sentence the reader does not need in order to act raises the cost of replying. Cut meta-commentary about why the event was good, cut bridges like "it got me thinking", cut anything the reader can already infer. But shortness serves the job; do not cut the goal-bearing content to hit a length.
- One true piece of yourself if you have it, but only if real. Never invent one to seem warm.
- Greet and sign as a person: first name in, first name out, no title block, no "let us keep in touch".
- Plain words, the way you actually speak. Read it back aloud as yourself; any phrase you would not say to this person's face is a tell. No em dash.
